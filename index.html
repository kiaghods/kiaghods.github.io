Mathematical Modeling of HIV and Protein Interactions

  • Skills: MATLAB, Mathematica

Human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) infection is a major public health concern with 1.2 million people living with HIV in the United States. The role of nutrition in general, and albumin/globulin in particular in HIV progression has long been recognized. However, no mathematical models exist to describe the interplay between HIV and albumin/globulin. Throughout this project, I collaborated with a team at Florida Atlantic University and University of Florida, where I worked on developing a set of ordinary differential equations describing these interactions, selected the best model from the set using the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), and conducted identifiability analysis on the model.

Expansion of Inforna + Development of Covalent Binders of c-Myc mRNA

  • Technical Skills: Python, Unix
  • Lab Skills: Cell Culture, Chem-CLIP, Compound Mining and Treatment, Organic Synthesis, Liquid Chromatography Mass Spectrometer (LCMS), High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)

Throughout my senior year of high school, I worked as a research intern at The Herbert Wertheim UF Scripps Institute for Biomedical Innovation & Technology in Dr. Matthew Disney’s lab in the Department of Chemistry. During that period, I worked on a variety of projects, both computational and wet lab. Particularly, I would like to highlight my work expanding Inforna - a database matching small molecules to RNA secondary structural motifs - to include 194 new molecules and 82 new motifs. Further, during the latter half of my time in the lab, I spent significant time working on the synthesis of covalent binders that targeted c-Myc, the mRNA encoding for MYC protein, which is a transcription factor over expressed in greater than 70% of human cancers. NIH Summer Institute in Biostatistics & Data Science

  • Machine Learning, TensorFlow, Python R, SAS

In the summer of 2022, I was an REU intern as part of the Florida Summer Institute in Biostatistics and Data Science. With Professor Motta, I worked on developing a supervised machine learning model that would automate the prediction of the location of DNA transcription factors given an input sequence. Further, I was exposed to a whole host of prediction models, ultimately presenting on the efficacy of various prediction models for college success given high school data.
